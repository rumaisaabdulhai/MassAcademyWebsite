MIT Lemelson InvenTeams Grant

This summer, I led a project with 3 MAMS students to help high school students search, find, and apply to volunteer opportunities, summer programs, and internships that interest them. Our proposed solution is HelpOut: a mobile application on iOS and Android platforms that would serve as a single destination for high schoolers to effectively search, filter, and directly apply for programs they are passionate about. We developed many the Android mobile application features over the summer, conducted a design study to assess the need for such a platform, wrote a grant proposal, and reached out to high schools and organizations in Massachusetts for support. Our team was selected to be a finalist for the grant. We hope to continue the project during senior year. The presentation below gives an overview of the project and also features some demos.

Beaver Works Aerial Vehicle Racing

As a sophomore, I attended the 2019 Beaver Works summer program at MIT for 4 weeks. I learned how to pilot and program a quadcopter using Python, the Robot Operating System (ROS), and openCV. Every day we would attend seminars from industry leaders including Lincoln Labs and BAE Systems. My experience at Beaver Works inspired me to pursue my STEM I Project.

During the first week, we practiced safety protocol and flying the Intel quadcopter. For the second week, we competed in small challenges such as programming the quadcopter to fly in certain configurations and avoid AR-tagged obstacles. We were even able to trace the shape of Beaver mascot with a light mounted on the quadcopter and a long exposure camera (left).

For the third week, we learned how to use openCV for computer vision so we could get our drone to follow an LED-illuminated path. Finally, during the fourth week, we prepared for the final racing competition and programmed our quadcopter to follow an LED-illuminated path while avoiding AR-tagged obstacles. The left video is a snippet of our drone navigating the obstacle course autonomously. We also made a final presentation that gives an overview of our code.







International Math Modeling

At MAMS, I became passionate about Math Modeling. I developed a keen interest for solving these modeling problems as they tackled issues in the real world– from climate change and energy consumption to marketing and sales. In November 2019, I formed a team with 3 MAMS students to compete in the 2019 High School Math Contest in Modeling (HiMCM), where a total of 791 teams competed. You can read more about my HiMCM experience here. We were proud of our work and had a lot of fun while doing it. We were thrilled when our team US-9539 won the Finalist Prize. We wanted to see what other competitions were out there, and found we were qualified to compete in the 2020 International Mathematical Modeling Competition (IMMC). See the 2020 problem here.

Below is a demo of the simulation I developed in Python that models the products damaged for various store layouts. You can find the code here. We modeled population density, product density, obstruction of view from cashiers, and number of customers to develop a scoring system to predict the total number of damaged products in a flash sale. Although we were unable to work together in person, we are grateful we had the opportunity to collaborate virtually.



Below is the paper we submitted for the IMMC competition.



I also competed in the 14-hour MathWorks Math Modeling Challenge with 4 MAMS students this February. We created a model to determine the number and locations of charging stations along major U.S. trucking routes for an all-electric trucking industry, and had to prioritize which routes should be developed with electric charging infrastructure first.
